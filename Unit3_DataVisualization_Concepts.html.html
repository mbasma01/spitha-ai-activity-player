The Art of Visualization

To create meaningful charts, you must first understand the language of data.
We will master 4 key concepts before entering the lab.

1. Data Types

Numerical (The Ruler)

Values that measure quantity. You can do math on them (Sum, Average).

Examples: Age, Height, Price ($)

Categorical (The Bucket)

Values that describe qualities or labels. You sort them into groups.

Examples: City, Gender, Zip Code

Trap Alert: "Zip Codes" and "Phone Numbers" look like numbers, but they are labels. You would never calculate the "Average Phone Number". Therefore, they are Categorical.

2. Distribution

The Shape of Data

Distribution tells us how data is spread out. Are values clustered in the middle? Or bunched at one end?

Analogy

Imagine a crowd of people. Most are average height. A few are short, a few are tall. That's a Normal distribution.

Normal Distribution

Bell-shaped. Symmetrical.

Skewed Distribution

Leans to one side. (e.g., Income is skewed by billionaires).

3. Correlation

The Connection

How two variables move together.

Positive: Both go up together. (Study Time vs Grades)
Negative: One up, one down. (Speed vs Travel Time)

Correlation != Causation

Ice cream sales and shark attacks both go up in summer.
They correlate, but ice cream doesn't cause sharks!

4. Aggregation

The Summary

Raw data is too detailed (every single transaction). Visualizations often need summaries.

Operations: Sum, Mean, Count, Max

Granularity

How zoomed in are you?
Daily -> Monthly -> Yearly.
Low Granularity (Yearly) shows long trends. High Granularity (Daily) shows noise.
